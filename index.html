01 — About Me

Engineer · Researcher · Climber

I'm a graduate researcher at the Biomimetics and Dextrous Manipulation Lab (BDML) under Prof. Mark Cutkosky. My work explores how robots can sense and act in the underwater world — from biomimetic tactile sensors that mimic seal whiskers, to dexterous manipulation policies that stay robust in dynamic ocean currents.

My research sits at the intersection of bio-inspired sensing, intelligent control, and manipulation. I believe the most elegant robots are those that borrow from biology — compliant beams, distributed sensing, soft skins — and pair them with learning-based decision-making that's grounded in physics.

Before Stanford, I earned dual B.S. degrees in Mechanical Engineering from Illinois Institute of Technology (with an AI minor) and Ajou University in South Korea (1st in class). I've previously worked on computer vision research with Prof. Yan Yan and dielectric-elastomer-actuator research with Prof. Je-sung Koh.

03 — Current Research

What I'm Building Right Now

Two active projects at Stanford BDML — both pushing the limits of how robots sense and act underwater.

Project Lead · In Progress Target: ICRA 2027 submission

UMI-Underwater: Robust Manipulation in Dynamic Ocean Currents

Juhyun Jung (project lead) · with Prof. Shuran Song & Prof. Mark Cutkosky, Stanford University

I'm leading a new line of work that asks: how can an underwater robot achieve robust and energy-efficient manipulation in dynamic ocean currents? Building on the UMI-Underwater framework — a diffusion-policy approach to underwater manipulation trained without underwater teleoperation — I'm extending the system from static pool conditions to the chaotic regime of real ocean dynamics.

The core insight: existing underwater policies fail in two ways when currents hit — they overshoot and drift because their state estimation is fragile, and they keep trying to execute the original plan even when the body has been displaced. My approach addresses both: a depth + IMU based relative-pose estimator anchored to the target object, and a control stack that halts diffusion-policy inference during massive disturbances, hands control to a low-level posture-recovery controller, and resumes the manipulation task only once the body is stable.

Looking further ahead, I'm exploring flow-exploiting control — instead of fighting against ocean currents, leverage them. Real-time flow estimates feed into an MPC cost function, so the robot opportunistically uses currents to reach the target with minimum energy. Target tasks include cable routing and underwater connector mating, with planned field deployment at Hopkins Marine Station.

Whisker Array for Distributed Hydrodynamic Perception

BDML team · Advisor: Prof. Mark Cutkosky

Our published work demonstrated that a single bioinspired FBG whisker mounted on the nose of a small underwater vehicle is enough to detect a moving target and decide whether to go straight or turn. The natural next question: what happens when you have many?

I'm building a whisker array — multiple whiskers distributed around an ROV, each multiplexed on shared optical fibers — that recovers richer hydrodynamic structure: flow direction, vortex location, wake geometry, and contact discrimination. The goal is to give underwater robots a low-power, fully-passive perception channel that works where vision and acoustics fail (turbidity, particulates, low-light, ecologically sensitive areas).

Fiber Bragg Grating Whiskers for Bioinspired Hydrodynamic Perception on Underwater Robots

Hao Li*, Tianyu Tu, Siyue Yao, Ziyang Chang, Juhyun Jung, Xiaochi Xie, Long Yin Chung, Tian-Ao Ren, Genliang Chen, Mark R. Cutkosky*

Paper Figures

Click to enlarge

Harbor seals track hydrodynamic trails with their vibrissae, enabling passive perception of moving targets in dark or turbid water. Inspired by this capability, we present compact fiber Bragg grating (FBG) whiskers for underwater robots. Like seal whiskers, they have a non-uniform taper and elliptical cross-section.

Controlled towing experiments show a monotonic relative-flow response from 0.1 – 0.6 m/s, a strong reduction of self-induced oscillation compared to a cylindrical baseline, and a pronounced dependence on angle of attack. In a field deployment, a single front-mounted whisker enabled a small ROV to distinguish between continuing straight and executing a turn — correctly selecting the branch in 17 of 20 trials (85.0 %) from whisker signals alone.

I contributed to the experimental platform design, signal-processing pipeline, and underwater field testing.

06 — Life Beyond Research

Off the Lab

When I'm not running experiments, you'll probably find me on a climbing wall — chalk on hands, mind on the next hold.

Climbing as Motion Planning

Climbing feels surprisingly close to robotics. You scan the route, estimate the cost of each hold, and choose the path that gets you to the top with the lowest total effort. It's motion planning, embodied — a real-time optimization where the state space is your body and the dynamics are gravity, friction, and fatigue.

The best climbs, like the best controllers, are the ones that look effortless.
